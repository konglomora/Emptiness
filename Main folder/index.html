The first American in space

February 1971. Apollo 14 commander Alan Shepard, the first American in space, prepares to spend his second day on the moon. After a short breakfast, Shepard and lunar module pilot Ed Mitchell descend the ramp to spend an entire day in geological surveys on the lunar surface. They became the first crew to use a two-wheeled cart, a modular equipment transporter that they dragged along the lunar surface and quickly filled with rocks.
The couple were almost exhausted, climbing the slope of another crater, and the MCC wanted to return them to the descent vehicle by all means. However, Shepard had yet another task to complete.
"Houston," Shepard said, gripping the long handle of the device he used to collect rock samples, "you probably know what I have in my hand?" Everyone who was at that time in the control center stared at the video monitors. "It just so happened that I have a real iron here with me, the sixth number." (Iron is a metal type of golf club with a spade-shaped head. - Ed.)
Wasting no time, Shepard pulled a ball out of his pocket, threw it onto the lunar surface and stabbed it with an impromptu club. A fountain of dust rose, but the ball flew only a few meters. The second blow brought a much more satisfactory result: after describing an arc, the ball disappeared from sight.
Since then, there has been a debate about how far the ball flew. You can put an end to the dispute only by returning to the moon.

David Griggs and Jeff Hoffman

Astronauts David Griggs and Jeff Hoffman carried out an unplanned spacewalk to attach a fly swatter to a manipulator, while Ree Seddon attempted to revive the satellite. Alas, the antenna still refused to open.
However, not all was lost. During the next shuttle flight in August of the same year, astronauts James Van Hoffen and William Fischer managed to mount a new module on the satellite during their spacewalk and successfully put it into orbit.

Captain

This is our commander. He knows how to breathe in space without a spacesuit, so we made him a captain. The captain has full power on board the ship. In civil aviation, the captain is the commander of the aircraft. The law gives the captain of a seagoing vessel very great powers, he acts as a judge and bailiff. The captains also traditionally performed religious rites, conducted the Divine Liturgy on board, crowned and buried the dead. In many cases, even a person senior in rank or position was not allowed to give orders to bypass the captain. According to the unwritten law of the sea, the captain had to leave the ship last.

Spaceship

A manned spacecraft designed to carry out flights of people in outer space and, in particular, to deliver people into space and their safe return to Earth (or another planet / satellite / space station). The first manned spacecraft was the Soviet spacecraft Vostok-1, on which Yuri Gagarin made the first full-fledged space flight, circling the Earth at the first space speed. One of the main problems in the design of this class of spacecraft is the creation of a safe, reliable and accurate system for returning the crew to the earth's surface in the form of a wingless descent vehicle (SA) or a spaceplane. In addition, an important feature is the presence of an emergency rescue system (SAS) at the initial stage of launching by a launch vehicle (LV). The projects of the first generation spacecraft did not have a full-fledged rocket SAS - instead of it, as a rule, ejection of the crew seats was used, the winged spaceplanes are also not equipped with a special SAS. Also, the spacecraft must be equipped with a life support system (LSS) for the crew. Launch of the Soyuz-TMA manned spacecraft to the International Space Station.

Astronaut

The concept of space flight is different in different countries. According to the classification of the International Federation of Aeronautics (FAI), space flight is considered to be a flight whose altitude exceeds 100 km. According to the classification of the United States Air Force (United State Air Forces, USAF), a space flight is a flight whose altitude exceeds 50 miles (80 km 467 m). In Russia, space flight is called an orbital flight, that is, a flight in which the device must make at least one revolution around the Earth. That is why the total number of astronauts in different sources differs.

Universe

The universe is not a rigidly defined concept in astronomy and philosophy. It is divided into two fundamentally different entities: speculative (philosophical) and material, accessible to observation at the present time or in the foreseeable future. If the author distinguishes between these entities, then, following tradition, the first is called the Universe, and the second - the astronomical Universe or Metagalaxy (recently this term has practically gone out of use). Historically, various words have been used to denote "all space", including equivalents and variants from different languages, such as "space", "world", "celestial sphere". The term "macrocosm" has also been used, although it is intended to define large-scale systems, including their subsystems and parts. Likewise, the word "microcosm" is used to refer to small scale systems. Any research, any observation, whether it is the observation of a physicist at how the nucleus of an atom is breaking, a child at a cat, or an astronomer observing a distant galaxy - all this is an observation of the Universe, or rather, of its individual parts. These parts serve as the subject of study of individual sciences, and astronomy and cosmology are engaged in the Universe on the largest possible scale, and even the Universe as a whole; in this case, the Universe is understood as either the region of the world covered by observations and space experiments, or the object of cosmological extrapolations - the physical Universe as a whole.
